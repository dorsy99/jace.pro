<p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="line-height: 26.6667px; font-size: 12pt;">Design is where the rubber-meets-the-road.   We have our <a title="" _jive_internal="true" href="/groups/servicenow-user-group-us-tx-north-texas/blog/2015/12/08/community-code-snippets-requirements-best-practices-for-developers">requirements</a>.   We have investigated and <a title="" _jive_internal="true" href="/groups/servicenow-user-group-us-tx-north-texas/blog/2015/12/12/community-code-snippets-analysis-and-prototype-best-practices-for-developers">analyzed </a>each of these and turned over all the technical rocks that might impede our progress.   We are now ready for design!</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><img  class="image-9 jive-image" src="e61bb739db54d3049c9ffb651f96197a.iix" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 620px; height: 147px;"/></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">This is another of the software engineering steps that I often see get tossed aside.   What appears to happen is that the requirements are gathered, and coding begins.   Almost at the same time!   This is similar, in my mind, to the old joke where a manager tells his developers to start coding, and he will go find out what the users want!</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Design is where you, as the developer, take the requirements, and analysis, and begin detailing and organizing how they are to be developed into a solution.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">My rule-of-thumb:   When I am done with the design I should be able to hand all the documentation to a junior developer who should be able to make ALL of the changes, and do the unit testing with a minimum of supervision.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Also, even after you get into the Design Phase it is possible to find a process or design problem that will require the team revisit the requirements, and/or analysis steps.   Each of these steps acts as checks-and-balances to reduce risk and improve quality.   Take them seriously!   They are not just bureaucratic make-work!</span></p><p><span style="font-size: 12pt;"> </span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">A side note:   The combination of Requirements, Analysis &amp; Prototyping, and Design should constitute roughly 60% of the time of a project.   The remaining 40% could and should be roughly divided in two and allotted to coding and testing.   Frankly, by the the time you finish with Design, coding should be a snap.   You have overcome your technical difficulties in prototyping, and with design you should have all of the to-dos, how-tos, in-what-orders, and unit tests defined.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">BTW, I do not limit prototyping to the big stuff.   Nor is it something that I relegate to the Analysis step only.   I will be working with code-snippets and using Scripts - Background or Fix Scripts throughout the project.   Don't be too rigid with the process.   Use common sense.   <span __jive_emoticon_name="happy" __jive_macro_name="emoticon" class="jive_macro_emoticon jive_emote jive_macro" data-renderedposition="686.7534790039062_770.2083740234375_16_16" src="/8.0.1.35b65d4/images/emoticons/happy.png"></span></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Contents</strong></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">What the Design Should Contain:</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">1. Detail on where changes will be made and what they will consist of.   Here is where coding standards can begin to dictate how the solution will look behind-the-scenes.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">2. The order in which the changes will be accomplished.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">3. The unit tests to be used to exercise all of the changes.   Yes, this will contain the detail and steps of the unit tests!</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">4. Any integration tests identified by the developer to ensure that the changes do not break the existing codebase.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="color: #666666; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;">I am not terribly locked into a rigid format on this.   I use common sense to achieve the purpose.   Remember:   Don't think of this as make-work!   It is truly an important step on the process.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Document Organization</strong></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">What follows is how I usually organize my design.   </span><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Note the order:   Each of these can have cumulative dependencies on the previous.   In addition, starting with table changes, there should be a reference to a requirement that is addressed by the change.   Some of these may address more than one requirement.   This is where requirements numbering shines as it is a way of specifically showing which requirement was addressed.   This is another facet of software engineering:   requirements traceability.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">1. Overview - a high-level "executive" description of the tasks that are to be accomplished with this design.   Include any referenced requirements documentation.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">For example:</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><img  class="image-5 jive-image" src="f64e1482db985344e9737a9e0f9619a3.iix" style="width: 620px; height: 255px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">2. Next in "constructive" order the design elements.   What this means is that each element build or relies on the previous.   </span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">For example:   Table changes should come before form changes.   You would add new columns to a table before adding those fields to a form.   So it is pretty much common sense.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">           This list is a good rule of thumb, but by no means is it inclusive.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">a. Table<span style="color: #666666; font-family: arial, sans-serif;"> - these will include new tables, column changes, column additions, ACL additions and changes</span></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">b. Forms<span style="color: #666666; font-family: arial, sans-serif;"> - these will include field locations on the form, label changes, UI Actions and ordering.   Here I pull in the screen shots from the requirements.</span></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">c. Database Views<span style="line-height: 1.5;"> - these will include new views, modifications to existing views.</span></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="line-height: 1.5; font-size: 12pt;">d. Code changes - here is where the underlying code is detailed.   This includes Client Scripts, Business Rules, Events and Script Actions, Script Includes, etc.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">e. Workflow changes - Diagram this using Visio or something like it.   Roughly what the workflow will be doing.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">f. Service Catalog and/or Record Producer changes</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 60px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">i. Storyboarding - How will the Service Catalog or Record Producer actually flow.   What will be on each page.   The variables involved.   What will be passed to the underlying workflow.   What client script or ui action will be involved.   This has a dependency on the workflow.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">g. Scheduled Jobs, Reporting, and other modifications that rely on everything prior.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 60px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">i. Scheduled Jobs can have a dependency on Script Includes.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 60px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">ii. Reporting can have a dependency on Database Views, or table changes.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><img  class="jive-image image-8" src="e2db9c46db185344e9737a9e0f96198a.iix" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 620px; height: 387px;"/></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="line-height: 1.5; font-size: 12pt;">h. Navigation - Application Menus, Modules</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="line-height: 1.5; font-size: 12pt;">i. Unit tests.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 60px;"><span style="line-height: 1.5; font-size: 12pt;">i. Detail these out.   These will include what is being tested for (the requirement), and the steps to produce the test.   Expected results.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">j. Integration tests.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 60px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">i. Detail these out.   These will be to the area of ServiceNow that is affected by the change.   They are to verify no impact.   Include the steps to produce the test.   Expected results.</span></p><p style="padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">k. Process tests.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 60px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">i. These are to verify that the process flow (if any) has been achieved.   This is usually a requirements validation step.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 60px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">ii. Example:   </span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 90px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Service Catalog -&gt; Collects information (variables) -&gt; Workflow -&gt; Active Directory command -&gt; MID Server -&gt; Active Directory -&gt; Expected return.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><img  class="image-7 jive-image" src="6d5b74cedb101b04ed6af3231f96194b.iix" style="width: 620px; height: 317px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong style="font-family: inherit; line-height: 1.5; font-style: inherit;">Suggested Tools</strong></span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-family: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-weight: inherit; line-height: 1.5; font-size: 12pt;">This is by no means an exhaustive list, or an endorsement of any of these, but I find the following to be useful:</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-weight: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-family: inherit; font-size: 12pt;">Visio - One of my main tools.   With this I can do flows, use-cases, storyboarding, etc.   </span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-weight: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-family: inherit; font-size: 12pt;">MS Word, Libre Office, Open Office or any other document tool - I can do diagramming, and documenting here as well, it is just a bit more difficult.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-weight: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-family: inherit; font-size: 12pt;">Evernote - This is a super tool for cloud collaboration.   </span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-weight: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-family: inherit; font-size: 12pt;">OneNote - I am probably most comfortable with this for design documentation.   It allows for great network collaboration, and with Office 365 you have cloud collaboration available as well.</span></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-family: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-weight: inherit; line-height: 1.5; font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Design Wrapup</strong></span></p><p></p><p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Give the team the chance to feedback on the design.   Keep everyone in the loop (thus the need for collaboration).</span></p><p></p><p></p><p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">When you have completed your design the final step is to get sign-off from the business owner!   Do NOT move forward to development without this!   An email confirmation should suffice.</span></p><p></p><p></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-family: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-weight: inherit; font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Conclusion</strong></span></p><p><span style="font-family: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-weight: inherit; font-size: 12pt;"><strong> </strong></span></p><p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Design is an important part of the software engineering process.   It has the purpose of making development simpler by logically organizing the various tasks that will need to be performed to achieve the requested changes.   The detail provided is useful not only to the developer who will be doing the work, but to the business owner, project manager, business analyst, and test team. The entire team benefits from understanding what the solution will actually involve.   </span></p><p></p><p><span style="font-size: 12pt; line-height: 1.5;">If you have not already included this step in your projects; I would highly recommend doing so.   To me this is one of critical components of software development.   Remember software engineering reduces risk, and improves quality!</span></p><p></p><p style="font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666;"><span style="font-weight: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-family: inherit; font-size: 12pt;">Steven Bell</span></p><p></p><p></p><p style="margin-bottom: 0.0001pt; font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; background: white;"><span style="color: purple; font-weight: inherit; font-family: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-size: 12pt;"><strong>If you find this article helps you, don't forget to log in and "like" it!</strong></span></p><p></p><p style="margin-bottom: 0.0001pt; font-family: arial, sans-serif; color: #666666; background: white;"><span style="color: purple; font-weight: inherit; font-family: inherit; font-style: inherit; font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Also, if you are not already, I would like to encourage you to become a member of our blog!</strong></span></p>
